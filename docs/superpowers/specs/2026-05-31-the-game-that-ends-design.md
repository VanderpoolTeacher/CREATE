# Design Spec — "The Game That Ends"

**A presentation and illustrative curriculum for K-12 educators**

- **Date:** 2026-05-31
- **Presenter:** A college instructor with experience inside coding boot camps and alternate training models, working at the intersection of higher education and workforce development.
- **Audience:** K-12 educators and administrators.
- **Working title:** *The Game That Ends: What Students Need After the Last Checklist*
  ("The school game" is the recurring metaphor used inside the talk, not the formal title.)

---

## 1. Purpose & Outcome

**Primary goal:** A wake-up call. When the K-12 audience walks out, they should *see the gap* between what schooling currently produces and what college and the workforce actually require — and feel the urgency to act. The curriculum is illustrative: "here's what addressing this could look like," not a turnkey product.

**Not in scope:** A persuasive ask for adoption, a partnership pitch, or a ready-to-teach course. Those could follow later; this deliverable is awareness first.

---

## 2. Core Thesis

> Students have mastered **the school game** — a game of checking off boxes someone else defines, with prodding when they stall. College and the workforce reward the opposite: ambiguity, self-direction, organization, and the ability to talk about your own work. **We hand off students who are excellent at a game that ends the day they leave us** — and AI has just raised the stakes.

The frame is deliberately **generous**: it does not blame students or teachers. Students are genuinely good at the game we built for them. The problem is that it is the *only* game we've trained — and no tier of student escapes the cost.

---

## 3. The Five (now Six) Skill Cliffs

Where "the game" changes and students fall through — each drawn directly from the presenter's firsthand observation:

1. **Decomposition** — From a list of boxes to check → an ambiguous, multistep project they must break down themselves. Students who can follow a checklist flawlessly cannot take a document/requirement/project and turn it into ordered, doable steps.
2. **Work & time organization** — From one assignment → multiple concurrent projects with colliding deadlines. Includes the concrete, mundane gaps: not using a calendar to **define and protect** their time; not organizing work into folders.
3. **Working with ambiguity** — From a fixed spec → requirements that change as you learn along the way. Asking clarifying questions *early* instead of the night before.
4. **Initiative / self-starting** — From waiting to be prodded → getting started on their own. *This is the core struggle.* Investigating a problem and proposing a solution before stopping or waiting on someone else.
5. **Communication & presentation** — From doing work silently → standing up and presenting it. Students haven't had enough reps in front of a group; even the humble **note card** feels foreign.
6. **Digital & systems fluency** *(added)* — Comfort with abstraction: how software actually works and **where files live**. Some students don't know where files are stored on their own machine. Increasingly non-optional in the AI era (see §6).

---

## 4. Three Students, One Blind Spot

The gap shows up differently across tiers, but **no tier escapes it.**

- **The top performers** — Succeed with or without us; give them a spark of motivation and they run down the hill. *But* even the school-game champions are too reliant on checklists and 1:1 management. They can't protect their time, organize their files, or find where things live. **Their grades mask the dependency rather than disprove it.**
- **The low performers** — Require the most hand-holding. **The trap:** the more we hand-hold and task them out, the more we **train task-dependency** — making them excellent at the school game instead of teaching them to break a problem down and own it. Hand-holding is the school game in its purest, most addictive form.
- **The middle** — Where we struggle most, and the students most likely to head **straight into the workforce** rather than a lecture hall.

**The closing line of this movement:** We don't even always do right by the college-bound — and we serve the workforce-bound least of all. These are exactly the students the **workforce itself** is telling us lack these skills. The presenter's workforce vantage point makes this the talk's credibility peak.

---

## 5. Narrative Arc (the talk)

Hybrid structure: "The Gap" (problem → implication → hope) with a **composite student, "Maya,"** threaded through — specifically a *capable, workforce-bound middle student*, neither struggler nor star.

1. **Hook** — The scene: present a project, open the floor for questions… silence. Then the flood of questions arrives the night before it's due. The audience recognizes it instantly.
2. **The frame — "The School Game"** — Named generously. Give students a list of boxes, they'll check every one. That's a real skill. But it's the only game we've trained, and even its winners are dependent.
3. **The game ends** — The six skill cliffs (§3), each carried by a concrete story.
4. **Three students, one blind spot** — The tiers, the hand-holding-trains-dependency trap, the workforce-bound students we serve least (§4). *Emotional + credibility peak.*
5. **The ground is shifting: AI raises the floor** — (§6.)
6. **Why it matters** — Stakes, carried by Maya.
7. **"We know your hands are tied"** — Acknowledge the constraint: standards, testing, mandated pacing, and graduation requirements force K-12's hand, and there isn't room to bolt on more curriculum. *Empathy before advice — this earns the right to make suggestions.*
8. **What could help — weave it in, don't add it on** — Practical, low-lift strategies: embed these skills into work teachers *already* assign; simple frameworks, suggestions, and rubrics for teaching *and* assessing them (§7).
9. **Close** — "What if we taught the game that doesn't end?" A concrete, hopeful ask to the audience.

**Length/format:** 30-45 minute session, ~20-30 slides. Built first as a **markdown outline with speaker notes** (slide-by-slide); final slide tool (PowerPoint / Google Slides) chosen later.

---

## 6. The AI Dimension — "AI Raises the Floor"

The tool reshaping every workplace is, in effect, **software that builds more software.** Using it well demands a working mental model of how software is built and where things live. The student who doesn't know where a file is saved is the *least* equipped for the exact tool now redefining their future.

This turns the talk from "kids these days" into "the cost of inaction just went up." The durable skills (§3) plus basic digital fluency are no longer a nice-to-have — they are the **new price of admission.**

---

## 7. What Could Help — Weave It In, Don't Add It On

**This section is the real payoff of the talk.** The wake-up call (§1-6) earns the room's attention; the practical, low-lift toolkit here is what they take home and the true deliverable. Everything before it exists to make this land.

### 7a. The constraint we respect

K-12's hand is forced. Standards, testing, mandated pacing, and graduation requirements leave little room to bolt on new content — and this talk does **not** ask anyone to add a course. We name that reality out loud, *before* any suggestion. The point is not *more* curriculum; it's a different *way of holding* the work teachers already assign. Acknowledging the constraint is what earns the right to make suggestions at all.

### 7b. Weave-in strategies (the core offer)

The primary, lowest-lift move: surface these skills *inside* existing assignments rather than teaching them separately. Examples:

- Hand out the next project as a deliberately under-specified brief — and grade the **clarifying questions**, not just the deliverable.
- Require a one-paragraph "how I'll break this down and schedule it" plan *before* work starts, on an assignment you already give.
- Add a 90-second stand-and-explain to work students already do.
- Make "where it lives" part of the grade: students organize their submission in a named folder structure.

Each is a tweak to existing work, not a new unit.

### 7c. Simple frameworks & rubrics (the take-home tools)

Lightweight, reusable tools the audience can use Monday morning:

- A short **framework/checklist** students can apply to any ambiguous task (e.g., a decomposition prompt sequence: *What's being asked? What don't I know? What are the steps? What's first?*).
- **Single-page rubrics for teaching *and* assessing** each of the six skill clusters — so a skill that's currently invisible becomes nameable and gradeable *inside* assignments teachers already give.

### 7d. Durable-skills framework (one page)

Six clusters, drawn straight from the presenter's observations:

1. **Decomposition** — turning a big ambiguous thing into ordered, doable steps.
2. **Work & time organization** — managing the steps and the schedule across *multiple* projects; using a calendar to protect time; organizing files/folders.
3. **Working with ambiguity** — clarifying questions early; adapting to changing requirements.
4. **Initiative / self-starting** — getting going without being told; investigating before escalating.
5. **Communication & presentation** — speaking about your work; the humble note card.
6. **Digital & systems fluency** — how software works; where things live; comfort with abstraction; AI-readiness.

### 7e. Pedagogical thread — Gradual Release

Every unit should show how to dial support **down** over time — deliberately *withdrawing* scaffolding so students learn to start, decide, and self-correct on their own. This is the direct antidote to the hand-holding trap (§4). The curriculum's job is not to add more checklists; it's to teach students to write their own.

### 7f. Sample unit sketches (optional — for teachers who *do* have room)

For anyone with space to go further than the weave-in tweaks (§7b). Otherwise these are illustrative only.

- **"The Ambiguous Brief"** — A deliberately under-specified project; rewards clarifying questions up front. *(Decomposition + ambiguity.)*
- **"Many Plates Spinning"** — Concurrent projects with a built-in deadline conflict; uses a real calendar and file organization. *(Work & time organization.)*
- **"Stand & Deliver"** — Frequent, low-stakes presentation reps with note cards. *(Communication.)*
- **"Where Does It Live?"** *(optional 4th)* — A digital-fluency mini-unit: files, folders, and what's really happening when you use an app or an AI tool. *(Digital & systems fluency.)*

---

## 8. Stories & Evidence

Grounded in the presenter's **firsthand experience, framed vividly** — stories and concrete examples, no external research required. Source anecdotes already captured:

- The "school game": students follow a checklist flawlessly but stall on a multistep project.
- Silence when questions are invited up front; a flood of questions the night before a project is due.
- Presentation reps missing; note cards feel foreign.
- Multiple concurrent projects with conflicting timing; changing requirements discovered mid-stream.
- Students who can't use a calendar to protect their time, can't organize work into folders, don't know where files are stored on their own machine.

`[YOUR STORY]` prompts will mark any beat where a fresh anecdote would strengthen the moment.

---

## 9. Deliverables

**The headline deliverable — the take-home toolkit (the real goal):**

1. **Weave-in strategy guide** — a short, practical set of moves for embedding the six skills into assignments teachers already give (§7b). Low-lift, no new curriculum.
2. **Frameworks & rubrics** — a student-facing decomposition framework, plus single-page rubrics for *teaching and assessing* each of the six skill clusters (§7c). These are what the audience walks out holding.

**Supporting the toolkit:**

3. **Presentation outline** — markdown, slide-by-slide, with speaker notes (~20-30 slides for a 30-45 min session). The wake-up call that earns attention for the toolkit.
4. **Durable-skills framework** — one page, six clusters, with the gradual-release thread (§7d-7e).
5. **3-4 sample unit sketches** *(optional)* — for teachers with room to go beyond weave-in (§7f).

Final slide tool to be chosen after the outline is approved.
