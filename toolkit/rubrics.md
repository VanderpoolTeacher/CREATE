# Durable Skills Rubrics

**How to use these:** Apply each rubric to assignments you already give — score the skill alongside the content without adding new curriculum. Pick the one or two rubrics that match what a given project naturally asks students to do, and note the level in your gradebook the same way you note a writing trait. Over time, students see these skills are real, nameable, and worth practicing.

---

## 1. Decomposition

| Criterion | Emerging | Developing | Independent |
|-----------|----------|------------|-------------|
| **Breaks a project into steps without a checklist** | Waits for teacher to provide a step-by-step list before starting; cannot name next action on their own. | Can identify a few steps when prompted, but order is incomplete or missing key pieces. | Independently maps out an ordered sequence of steps before asking for help; adjusts the plan when something changes. |
| **Sequences steps in a logical order** | Lists tasks randomly or all at once with no sense of what must come first. | Puts most steps in a workable order but skips dependencies (e.g., writing before researching). | Steps are sequenced so each one sets up the next; identifies which steps are blockers. |
| **Estimates what each step will take** | No sense of relative effort; treats all tasks as the same size. | Identifies big vs. small tasks but doesn't connect estimates to available time. | Sizes each step well enough to decide where to start and how to pace the work. |

## 2. Work & Time Organization

| Criterion | Emerging | Developing | Independent |
|-----------|----------|------------|-------------|
| **Uses a calendar to plan and protect work time** | Does not use a calendar; all planning is in their head or not at all. | Has deadlines written down but doesn't block time in advance to do the work. | Adds working sessions to a calendar before deadlines arrive; notices when two projects collide and adjusts. |
| **Organizes files so they can be found again** | Files are untitled, unsaved in a default location, or simply lost; cannot locate own work without a search. | Files are saved but named inconsistently; finding them takes trial and error. | Files are named clearly and stored in a folder structure they can describe to someone else; nothing important is lost. |
| **Tracks tasks across multiple projects** | Loses track of what's due across more than one class or project at a time. | Tracks due dates for individual assignments but misses that two land on the same day. | Maintains a running list of open tasks across all projects and updates it regularly. |

---

## 3. Working with Ambiguity

| Criterion | Emerging | Developing | Independent |
|-----------|----------|------------|-------------|
| **Asks clarifying questions early in a project** | Goes silent when a project is introduced; sends a flood of questions the night before it's due. | Asks some questions early but waits until stuck rather than front-loading key clarifications. | Within the first day or two of a project, asks targeted questions that remove the biggest unknowns before starting. |
| **Makes a reasonable assumption when information is incomplete** | Freezes or refuses to proceed without explicit instructions for every step. | Will move forward when told it's okay, but does not flag the assumption made. | States an assumption out loud ("I'm going to treat X as meaning Y — let me know if that's wrong") and keeps working. |
| **Adapts when requirements shift** | Treats a change in scope or direction as a crisis; restarts from scratch or shuts down. | Adjusts the work but expresses significant frustration and needs reassurance before continuing. | Acknowledges the change, identifies which parts of existing work still apply, and updates the plan without drama. |

## 4. Initiative / Self-Starting

| Criterion | Emerging | Developing | Independent |
|-----------|----------|------------|-------------|
| **Starts work without being prompted** | Sits idle after a project is assigned; waits to be told the specific next step before doing anything. | Gets started after a general nudge ("you should probably begin") but needs that nudge every session. | Opens the project and begins on their own, without a reminder, at the expected time or earlier. |
| **Investigates a problem before asking for help** | Asks teacher the first question that arises without attempting to find the answer independently. | Makes one attempt to find an answer, then asks immediately if it doesn't work. | Makes a genuine effort to investigate (tries a different approach, searches, re-reads), then asks a specific, informed question. |
| **Proposes a next step when stuck** | Reports being stuck and waits for the teacher to provide the next move. | Says what the problem is but offers no idea for how to proceed. | Describes the problem and offers at least one concrete idea for what to try next. |

## 5. Communication & Presentation

| Criterion | Emerging | Developing | Independent |
|-----------|----------|------------|-------------|
| **Presents work to a group** | Reads directly from a screen or sheet with no eye contact; audience cannot follow the explanation. | Makes occasional eye contact and has a loose structure, but loses the thread partway through. | Looks at the audience, follows a clear arc (what I did, why, what I learned), and finishes within time. |
| **Uses notes or note cards as a guide, not a script** | Either reads every word verbatim or has nothing and improvises with no structure. | Uses notes but drifts into reading them word-for-word when nervous. | Glances at notes to stay on track but speaks in their own words; note cards hold cues, not sentences. |
| **Explains their own decisions, not just their output** | Describes what the project is without explaining why any choices were made. | Names a choice or two when prompted but cannot explain the reasoning unprompted. | Volunteers the reasoning behind at least one key decision; can answer "why did you do it that way?" without hesitation. |

## 6. Digital & Systems Fluency

| Criterion | Emerging | Developing | Independent |
|-----------|----------|------------|-------------|
| **Knows where files live and how storage works** | Cannot locate files on their own device; doesn't distinguish between local storage, cloud, and downloads folder. | Can find a file eventually but describes storage in vague terms ("it's saved somewhere"). | Can explain where a file is saved and why (local vs. cloud, which folder, which account) and retrieve it immediately. |
| **Describes how a piece of software does its job** | Treats software as a black box; has no model for what happens between input and output. | Can describe the general purpose of a tool but not how data moves through it. | Gives a simple, accurate model of what happens: input goes in, it is processed this way, output comes out. |
| **Recovers when technology doesn't behave as expected** | Stops working and waits for someone else to fix the problem. | Tries one thing (usually restarting) and escalates immediately if it doesn't work. | Tries a small set of logical steps, narrows down the likely cause, and knows when to escalate with a specific description of what was tried. |

---

*These rubrics are meant to sit alongside your existing grading, not replace it. If a student aces the content but scores Emerging on Initiative, that gap is worth naming — because in the next classroom or the next job, the content won't matter if they can't get themselves started.*
