# Weave-In Strategies

## The constraint we respect

K-12 teachers operate inside a structure built from the outside in: standards, mandated pacing, high-stakes testing, graduation requirements. There is no margin for a new course, a new unit, or another initiative. That is real, and this document does not pretend otherwise. What it asks for instead is a different way of holding the work you already assign — same assignment, same time slot, same grade, different framing. Every move below fits inside what you are already doing.

---

## The Decomposition Framework

Post this on the wall. Hand it out at the start of any complex assignment. Students apply these four questions to whatever task is in front of them — no new curriculum required.

> **Before You Start — Four Questions**
>
> 1. **What's actually being asked?** Say it in one sentence, in your own words.
> 2. **What do I not know yet?** List every unknown — information gaps, unclear terms, missing constraints.
> 3. **What are the steps?** Break the work into chunks small enough to hand to a calendar.
> 4. **What's first?** Identify the blocker — the one thing everything else depends on.

This sequence is the cognitive move that separates students who get started from students who wait. Use it on the assignment they are already doing.

## Weave-In Moves by Cluster

### 1. Decomposition

**The "plan-before-you-start" memo**
Before students touch the actual work, require a one-paragraph written plan: how they will break this project down and when they intend to do each part. No more than half a page. Grade it alongside the finished product.

- Fits onto: any multi-day assignment, research paper, lab report, project.
- Assess with: the **Decomposition** rubric in `rubrics.md` — specifically "break a project into ordered steps" and "identify which step must come first."

**The under-specified brief**
Hand out the next project as a deliberately sparse prompt — less detail than usual. Make the clarifying questions students ask part of the grade, not just the deliverable. A student who asks three sharp, early questions before starting is demonstrating exactly the right instinct.

- Fits onto: any project you already assign; just remove one or two specification details from the handout.
- Assess with: the **Decomposition** rubric ("identify which step must come first") and the **Working with Ambiguity** rubric ("ask clarifying questions early").

---

### 2. Work & Time Organization

**"Where it lives" is part of the grade**
When students submit work, require it to be organized in a named folder structure you specify (or that they define and label). A submission that cannot be found or that lives in "Downloads" does not demonstrate the skill.

- Fits onto: any digital submission — essay, slideshow, spreadsheet, video file.
- Assess with: the **Work & Time Organization** rubric ("organize files so work can be found again") and the **Digital & Systems Fluency** rubric ("locate where a file lives").

**The deadline collision check**
Before any multi-week project, give students five minutes to write down every other due date or commitment they have during the same window. Ask: do any collide? What do you do about it? This is time-management thinking made visible.

- Fits onto: the opening day of any unit with a major project.
- Assess with: the **Work & Time Organization** rubric ("use a calendar to protect work time" and "track open tasks across more than one project at a time").

---

### 3. Working with Ambiguity

**Grade the questions, not just the answers**
When you hand out the under-specified brief (see Decomposition above), tell students explicitly: "The questions you ask me in the first two days count toward your grade." Give partial credit for a substantive clarifying question asked early. Give no credit for questions asked the night before it is due.

- Fits onto: any project already in your rotation.
- Assess with: the **Working with Ambiguity** rubric — "ask clarifying questions early, not the night before something is due."

**State your assumption**
On any assignment where students must interpret or estimate something, require them to write one sentence: "I am assuming X because Y." That single sentence is the whole move.

- Fits onto: math word problems, science lab write-ups, essay prompts with open-ended scope.
- Assess with: the **Working with Ambiguity** rubric ("make a stated assumption and keep working when information is incomplete").

---

### 4. Initiative / Self-Starting

**The silent first step**
At the start of a work session, before you give any direction, have students write down — silently and independently — the first thing they plan to do today on their project. Collect or share out after two minutes. The move makes self-starting a public, practiced habit.

- Fits onto: the opening two minutes of any class that includes project work time.
- Assess with: the **Initiative / Self-Starting** rubric ("start work without being prompted").

**The "what I tried first" box**
Add a small box to any assignment sheet: "Before asking for help, I tried: ___." Students fill it in. If the box is blank and the hand goes up, redirect: "Fill in the box first." Over time, the box trains the disposition.

- Fits onto: any worksheet, lab, or project handout — one added line.
- Assess with: the **Initiative / Self-Starting** rubric ("investigate a problem before asking for help").

---

### 5. Communication & Presentation

**The 90-second stand-and-explain**
At the end of a work session or after a quiz, call on a few students to stand and explain — in 90 seconds — what they did, why they made a key choice, and what they would do differently. No slides, no preparation beyond the work they just did.

- Fits onto: after any assignment students have already completed — lab, essay draft, math problem set, art critique.
- Assess with: the **Communication & Presentation** rubric — "present work to a group clearly and within time" and "explain the reasoning behind their decisions."

**The one-sentence summary**
Before a student turns in any written assignment, require a single closing sentence: "The most important thing I want you to understand about this work is ___." It forces them to think about what they are actually communicating, not just what they produced.

- Fits onto: any essay, report, or project with a written component.
- Assess with: the **Communication & Presentation** rubric ("explain the reasoning behind their decisions, not just the output").

---

### 6. Digital & Systems Fluency

**Name the tool, describe the move**
Once per unit, ask students to write two sentences about a digital tool they used: what it does, and how information moves through it. Not a how-to guide — a mental model. "I used Google Slides. I put text and images into it, it arranged them on a page, and the output was a shareable link."

- Fits onto: any assignment that involves a digital tool — word processing, spreadsheets, presentation software, a learning management system.
- Assess with: the **Digital & Systems Fluency** rubric ("describe a basic mental model of how a piece of software does its job").

**The "where it lives" requirement** is also listed here — see Work & Time Organization above. Both rubric clusters apply, and both can be assessed on the same submission.

---

## Closing

Every move above is a small withdrawal of scaffolding — and that is the whole point. The aim is to release responsibility gradually until students are the ones who decide what to do first, catch their own gaps, and correct their own course without waiting to be told. This toolkit exists to work itself out of a job. We are not asking students to follow our checklist forever; we are building the habit of writing their own.
