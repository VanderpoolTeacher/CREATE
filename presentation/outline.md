### Slide 2 — Silence in the Room
**On slide:** A project brief on screen. "Any questions?" Then: one word — *Silence.*
**Notes:** Picture this. I hand a class a project — the whole thing, up front. I walk them through it. Then I do the thing we're all trained to do: "Okay — any questions?" And there is silence. How do I even start?" Meet Maya. Maya is real to me — a composite of a hundred students. She's capable. She's not a struggler and she's not a star; she's right in that big, important middle, and she's heading straight into the workforce. Maya didn't ask a single question that first day. ### Slide 3 — They're Good at a Game
**On slide:** A checklist with every box ticked. ✓ ✓ ✓ ✓
**Notes:** Here's what I want to say generously, and I mean it: our students are good. Genuinely good — at a specific game. Give them an explicit list of boxes, and they will check every single one. That is a real skill. It takes discipline, it takes follow-through, it's not nothing. We built that game, and we built it well, and they learned to win it. I'm not here to blame students, and I'm certainly not here to blame teachers. The game works exactly as designed. The problem isn't that they're bad at it. The problem is it's the only game we ever taught them.

### Slide 4 — Even the Winners
**On slide:** "Even the winners are dependent."
**Notes:** And here's the part that should give us pause. It's not just the kids who struggle. Even the *winners* of the school game — the checklist champions, the ones with the spotless transcripts — even they are too reliant on the checklist and on someone managing them one-on-one. I've watched school-game winners who can't use a calendar to protect their own time. Can't organize their work into folders. Some of them genuinely don't know where a file lives on their own computer. These are our success stories. So when I say the game ends, I'm not talking about a few stragglers. I'm talking about almost everyone.

### Slide 5 — The Game Ends
**On slide:** "College and the workforce hand you no boxes."
**Notes:** Because here's the thing about the game we built: it ends. It ends the day they leave us. College hands you no boxes. The workforce hands you no boxes. Nobody walks up to Maya at her first job and says, "Here's your itemized checklist for the quarter." They say, "Here's a problem. Figure it out." And the skills that make that possible — the durable ones — are exactly the ones we never put on a checklist. I want to walk you through six places where the ground drops out. Six cliffs.

### Slide 6 — Cliff 1: Decomposition
**On slide:** **Decomposition** — break an ambiguous multistep project into ordered steps.
**Notes:** Cliff one: Decomposition. Hand a student an explicit checklist and they execute beautifully. But hand them a document, a requirement, a multistep project and ask *them* to break it down — and they stall. The skill of taking something big and ambiguous and turning it into "okay, first I do this, then this" — we assume it's automatic. It isn't. We've been doing the decomposition *for* them for thirteen years. The day we stop, the cliff appears. Here's what it looks like for me: in the design space, a project means investigate → get feedback → prototype → get feedback → finalize → present. That's six moves they have to sequence themselves. I spend the first two years just getting students comfortable with that process — it's only by year three or four, and only for some, that I can take the training wheels off.

### Slide 7 — Cliff 2: Work & Time Organization
**On slide:** **Work & Time Organization** — multiple projects; a calendar that protects time; files that have a home.
**Notes:** Cliff two: Work and Time Organization. Real work is never one project at a time. It's three or four concurrent projects with colliding deadlines. And our students have mostly experienced one assignment, then the next, then the next — neatly queued. They've never had to protect their own time on a calendar, juggle competing priorities, or keep their files organized so they can find their own work next week. I mentioned the file thing already — it keeps coming up, and it's going to come up again, because it matters more than it sounds.

### Slide 8 — Cliff 3: Working with Ambiguity
**On slide:** **Working with Ambiguity** — ask clarifying questions early; adapt when requirements change.
**Notes:** Cliff three: Working with Ambiguity. This is the silence from the opening. In real work, requirements change as you learn along the way — that's not a failure, that's Tuesday. The professional move is to ask clarifying questions early, while there's still time to use the answers. Our students wait. They go quiet. And the questions arrive the night before, when it's too late to change anything. We've trained them to treat ambiguity as something to endure quietly instead of something to interrogate out loud. I do this to my students on purpose — I build ambiguity into the prompt, because asking the questions *is* the skill. **The Dean story:** a couple years after COVID I gave juniors at Bowling Green an ambiguous brief ("build a website for X, fill in the blanks"), a student complained up to the acting Dean, and the Dean told me these kids came from high-school-and-COVID checklists and we "need to support that." In my gut: that's exactly backwards. [live, you said "that's bull" — clean version lands just as hard for an admin room.] **But the hopeful half:** these things *can* be taught. The students I had as freshmen and sophomores — trained to ask, collaborate, think without a checklist — outperform the third- and fourth-years who never dealt with ambiguity. Same degree; the only difference is when they started getting reps.

### Slide 9 — Cliff 4: Initiative / Self-Starting
**On slide:** **Initiative / Self-Starting** — get going without being prodded.
**Notes:** Cliff four, and to me this is the core of the whole thing: Initiative. Self-starting. Getting going without being prodded. Investigating a problem and proposing a solution — instead of stopping, or waiting for someone to come tell you what to do next. This is the muscle the workforce wants most and sees least. Maya can finish anything you start her on. The question her boss has is: can she start? Can she look at a problem nobody assigned and decide to move on it?

### Slide 10 — Cliff 5: Communication & Presentation
**On slide:** **Communication & Presentation** — speak about your own work. (A single note card.)
**Notes:** Cliff five: Communication and Presentation. And folks, this one is getting worse every day — anecdotally and backed by data — and it's hitting our young men the hardest. When I ask students to actually stand up and present their own work, it's clear they haven't had enough reps standing in front of a group. And here's a small detail that tells the whole story — the idea of using note cards feels *foreign* to them. Back up even further: students show up with no pencil, no paper, just a Chromebook — and trust me, as a designer out in the field, the most successful people I know still reach for pencil and paper. They've never been taught the basic craft of talking about what they made: a few notes on a card, look up, explain it. It's not stage fright. It's that we never gave them the practice. And nearly every job on earth eventually asks you to explain your work to another human — and explaining your own work confidently is the one thing AI *cannot* do for them. AI can hand them the product; it can't hand them the process or the ability to own it.

### Slide 11 — Cliff 6: Digital & Systems Fluency
**On slide:** **Digital & Systems Fluency** — how software works; *where files live.*
**Notes:** Cliff six: Digital and Systems Fluency. A basic mental model of how software works and where things actually live. And yes — back to the file that has no home. The student who doesn't know where a file is stored on their own machine. We used to file this under "they'll pick it up" — and here's the trap in that: we assume that because they're good *users* of technology, they'll be good *builders* of it. That's the farthest thing from the truth. Try it: ask a student what's inside a cell phone — they can't tell you. Ask them to set up a folder structure for the six or seven classes they're taking — watch how many can't. Hold that thought, because in a few minutes I'm going to tell you why this particular cliff just became the most dangerous one of all.

### Slide 12 — Three Students
**On slide:** Three tiers: Top / Middle / Bottom. A spotlight on the Middle.
**Notes:** Now I want to complicate the picture honestly, because not every student meets these cliffs the same way. In my experience there are roughly three tiers. And once you see them, you can't unsee where our energy actually goes.

### Slide 13 — The Top and the Bottom
**On slide:** Top: "a spark, and they run down the hill." Bottom: "the most hand-holding — and the trap."
**Notes:** The top performers succeed with us or without us. Honestly — give them a spark of motivation and they run down the hill on their own. They're going to be fine. Then there are the students who struggle most, and they rightly get the most hand-holding. But here's the trap, and it's a painful one: the more we hand-hold, the more we task them out into tiny steps, the more we are actually *training task-dependency*. We think we're helping them break down the problem. We're doing the breaking-down *for* them — again and again — and teaching them that someone else always will. We're training the exact dependency that ends them at the cliff. *[OPTIONAL ASIDE — read the room: the student-athletes who had someone help them stay eligible and now need every step validated one-on-one — and the same "confirm me at every step" dependency you've seen in some high-flyers, too. The thing that drives managers crazy. Cut cleanly if the room's not with you.]*

### Slide 14 — The Middle, and Our Blind Spot
**On slide:** "The middle is where we struggle most — and they go straight to work."
**Notes:** And then there's the middle. The middle is where we struggle most to reach, because they don't demand our attention the way the top and the bottom do. They're quiet. They're fine. They're Maya. And here's the gut-punch: the middle is the group most likely to head straight into the workforce. The students who will most immediately need decomposition, initiative, and self-starting are the ones getting the least deliberate attention on exactly those skills. I'll be candid — this is the part I can't let go of. I've seen this from the workforce side, and the workforce itself is the one naming these as the skills students lack. That's not my opinion. That's the people doing the hiring.

### Slide 15 — The Ground Is Shifting
**On slide:** "AI raises the floor." A simple icon: software → builds → software.
**Notes:** And just as we're naming all this, the ground moves under us. I'll be honest about where students are: so many young people I meet passionately hate AI — "because it's taking jobs." So I get how hard it is to motivate them when they think the payoff is being pulled away. But that's not what we're doing. We're not preparing them for jobs — we're preparing them to be creative problem-solvers in a field they care about. And that I can guarantee: no matter the field, there will be problems, and the people who can solve them will always be in demand. Sometimes with a shovel, sometimes a welder, sometimes AI. Because the tool now reshaping every single workplace your students will enter is, in effect, software that builds more software. AI. It's already redefining what entry-level work even looks like.

### Slide 16 — The Cruelest Part
**On slide:** "The student who can't find a file is least ready for the tool redefining their future."
**Notes:** Here's the part that should make every one of us sit up. To use these AI tools *well* — not just type into a box, but actually direct them — you need a basic mental model of how software works and where things live. Which means the student who doesn't know where a file lives on their own computer is now the *least* equipped person in the room for the single tool redefining their future. The cliff I asked you to hold onto — Digital and Systems Fluency — AI didn't make it less important. It moved it from "nice to have" to "the floor." The floor just rose, and a lot of our students are standing below it.

### Slide 17 — Why This Matters
**On slide:** "This isn't about grades. It's about agency."
**Notes:** So let me say plainly why I think this matters — beyond test scores, beyond college acceptance. This is about whether our students get to be the authors of their own work and their own lives, or whether they spend their careers waiting for the next checklist that's never coming. It's about agency. The capacity to look at an unfamiliar, ambiguous situation and think, "I can break this down. I can start. I can ask the right question." That's not a soft skill. That's the whole game.

### Slide 18 — Back to Maya
**On slide:** "Maya is capable. The question is whether she knows it."
**Notes:** Come back to Maya with me. Maya is capable — I want to be clear about that. She's not lacking intelligence or work ethic. What she's lacking is practice at a different game. Right now, Maya's first day at her first real job is going to be the very first time anyone hands her a problem instead of a checklist. And that's an unfair place to do it for the first time — in front of a manager, with a paycheck on the line. We can give her those reps now, where the stakes are low and we're standing right beside her. The question isn't whether Maya is capable. It's whether she'll ever find out before it costs her.

### Slide 19 — We Know Your Hands Are Tied
**On slide:** "No room to add a course. We know."
**Notes:** Now — before I offer a single suggestion, I want to say something, and I want to say it sincerely. I know your hands are tied. I know about the standards you have to cover, the testing calendar, the mandates, the pacing guides. I am not standing up here, from outside your building, to tell you to add a course. There is no room. Your curriculum is full, and I respect that completely. So everything I'm about to give you is built on one constraint we're going to respect: nothing new gets *added*. It gets *woven in*. Empathy first, then the help.

### Slide 20 — Weave It In, Don't Add It On
**On slide:** "The constraint we respect: no new course. Tweak what you already assign."
**Notes:** Here's the core idea of the toolkit you're walking out with today. You already give rich, real assignments. We don't need new ones. We need small, low-lift tweaks to the assignments you already give — tweaks that turn a quiet checklist-execution exercise into a rep for decomposition, initiative, and the rest. Let me hand you the three pieces.

### Slide 21 — Toolkit Piece 1: The Durable-Skills Framework
**On slide:** **Durable-Skills Framework** — one page, six clusters.
**Notes:** Piece one: the Durable-Skills Framework. It's one page. It's the six clusters I just walked you through — Decomposition, Work and Time Organization, Working with Ambiguity, Initiative and Self-Starting, Communication and Presentation, and Digital and Systems Fluency. That's it. One page on your wall, or in your planning doc, so that when you design or tweak an assignment you can ask: which of these six is this a rep for? It's the shared language. Everything else hangs off it.

### Slide 22 — Toolkit Piece 2: Competency-Based Rubrics
**On slide:** **Competency-Based Rubrics** — "The student can…" · Not Yet → Approaching → Proficient → Advanced.
**Notes:** Piece two: Competency-Based Rubrics. These are written as plain "The student can…" statements — "The student can break an ambiguous brief into ordered steps." "The student can ask a clarifying question before starting." And each one is scored on a simple ladder: Not Yet, Approaching, Proficient, Advanced. The point is you don't grade these in a vacuum or in a new unit. You lay them over an assignment you *already* give. Same project, new lens. Now you can actually see and name growth on the skills that used to be invisible.

### Slide 23 — Toolkit Piece 3: Weave-In Strategies + Decomposition Framework
**On slide:** **Weave-In Strategies + Decomposition Framework** — low-lift moves for assignments you already give.
**Notes:** Piece three, and this is the practical heart of it: a menu of Weave-In Strategies, plus a Decomposition Framework students can actually use. These are the small moves. Let me show you a few concrete ones so you can picture it.

### Slide 24 — Weave-In Move: Grade the Questions
**On slide:** "Hand them an under-specified brief. Grade the clarifying questions."
**Notes:** Weave-in move number one — my favorite, because it directly attacks the opening silence. Take an assignment and deliberately *under-specify* it. Leave gaps. Then tell students up front: part of your grade is the clarifying questions you ask in the first ten minutes. Suddenly asking questions early isn't a sign of weakness to hide — it's the assignment. You've made Working with Ambiguity worth points. The silence breaks because you gave them permission and a reason to break it.

### Slide 25 — Weave-In Move: Plan Before You Start
**On slide:** "A 'plan-before-you-start' memo. Make 'where it lives' part of the grade."
**Notes:** Move two: before they touch the actual work, students hand in a short plan-before-you-start memo — their decomposition. "Here are the steps I'm going to take, in order." You're grading the thinking, not just the product, and you're forcing the initiative muscle: figure out *how* to start before you start. And tucked into the same memo — make "where it lives" part of the grade. Where is this file saved? What's it named? Which folder? It sounds almost too small to matter. It is the AI-era floor. Five points for knowing where your own work lives is five points well spent.

### Slide 26 — Weave-In Move: Stand and Explain
**On slide:** "A 90-second stand-and-explain. One note card."
**Notes:** Move three: the 90-second stand-and-explain. Not a formal presentation with slides — just stand up, one note card, ninety seconds, tell us what you made and why. Do it often enough that it stops feeling foreign. That's how the note card stops being a strange object and becomes a tool. Reps, low stakes, frequent. By the end of a semester, standing up to explain your own work is just a thing your students do — which is exactly what every workplace will eventually ask of them.

### Slide 27 — The Game That Doesn't End
**On slide:** "What if we taught the game that doesn't end?"
**Notes:** So here's the question I want to leave you sitting with. We are extraordinary at teaching the game that ends — the checklist game, and again, that's a real skill and I don't want to lose it. But what if, woven right into the assignments you already give, we also taught the game that *doesn't* end? The game of breaking down the unknown, starting without being asked, asking the brave question early, and knowing where your own work lives. None of it requires a new course. It requires a new lens on the work you're already doing.
