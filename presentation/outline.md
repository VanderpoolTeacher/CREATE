# Rebuilding the Foundations for Student Success

**Audience:** K-12 educators and administrators
**Format:** A lean resource walkthrough — the slides hold the *what* (the toolkit); the presenter brings the *why* and the stories live, in their own voice.
**Slides:** 8 · **Time:** flexible (~15–30 min depending on how much you talk around each slide)

> This deck is a scaffold, not a script. Each slide is sparse on purpose. The notes below are **talking-point prompts** — cues to riff from your own experience, not lines to read.

---

### Slide 1 — Title
**On slide:** *Rebuilding the Foundations for Student Success* · "Durable skills for college **and** the workforce" · Michael Vanderpool — Higher Ed & Workforce Development.
**Talk around:** Who you are — college instructor, coding bootcamps, workforce development; you see these students about 18 months after they leave K-12. What you keep seeing. Set up the session as practical: *here are resources you can use Monday — I'll walk the what, you bring the why.*

### Slide 2 — The one idea
**On slide:** "Prepare them for college **and** the workforce — woven into what you already teach."
**Talk around:** Not everyone goes to a four-year college — trades, military, two-year, straight to work — and the durable skills are the same either way. Nothing new gets added; it gets woven into assignments you already give.

### Slide 3 — The six durable skills
**On slide:** Decomposition · Work & time organization · Working with ambiguity · Initiative / self-starting · Communication & presentation · Digital & systems fluency.
**Talk around:** Riff on each from real experience — the student who can't break a project into steps, can't find their own file, won't ask the question until 11 p.m., freezes on an ambiguous brief, has never stood up to explain their work, treats software as magic. Especially the workforce-bound middle of the room.

### Slide 4 — Resource 1: The Framework
**On slide:** **1** — The Durable-Skills Framework · "One page. The six skills, defined plainly. Your shared language."
**Talk around:** Pin it on the wall or in your planning doc. When you design or tweak an assignment, ask: *which of these six is this a rep for?*

### Slide 5 — Resource 2: Rubrics
**On slide:** **2** — Competency Rubrics · "The student can…" · Not Yet → Approaching → **Proficient** → Advanced.
**Talk around:** Lay it over an existing assignment — same project, new lens. Now you can name growth on skills that used to be invisible. Proficient = demonstrated and independent (the target); Advanced = extends it unprompted.

### Slide 6 — Resource 3: Weave-In Moves
**On slide:** **3** — Weave-In Moves: grade the clarifying questions · plan-before-you-start memo · 90-second stand-and-explain · make "where it lives" part of the grade.
**Talk around:** The low-lift tweaks. Pick a few and walk how each turns a quiet checklist task into a rep. Tell the story of the silence breaking once asking questions is worth points.

### Slide 7 — Resource 4: Turnkey Units
**On slide:** **4** — Turnkey Units · "Five ready-to-teach units — lesson plans, printable student handouts, and rubrics. For when there's room to go further."
**Talk around:** Optional path — weave-in is primary. But if a teacher has room, these are pick-up-and-teach: sessions, timing, handouts, look-fors, gradual release.

### Slide 8 — Where to get it all (close)
**On slide:** "It's all on one page." · QR code · vanderpoolteacher.github.io/CREATE.
**Talk around:** Framework, rubrics, weave-in moves, and the units — plus this deck — all live there. Scan and it's yours. Close in your own words with the small ask: *pick one assignment, weave in one move, this semester* — the reps add up. Thank them.
